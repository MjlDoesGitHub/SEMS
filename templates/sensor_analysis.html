Sensor Analysis

MQ135 Sensor: Air Quality

High sensitivity to NH3 gas, H2S, benzene series

Semiconductor type: Has a steel exoskeleton under where the sensing element is housed . The sensing element is subjected to current. The gases that come close to the sensing element are ionized and absorbed by the sensing element which changes the sensing element’s resistance and alters the value of the current going out of it

Uses SnO2, a gas sensitive material that has a higher resistance in clean air. Increase in number of harmful gasses decreases the resistance of the gas MQ135.

Analog output signal is proportional to the gas vapor concentration with is measured by the MQ135 sensor module.

Optimal Working Conditions:

  • Temp: -10 to 45C
  • Humidity < 95% 11
  • Operates a 5V with a 150mA consumption
  • 20 sec preheating is required to obtain accurate output

    Electrons absorb energy from radiation can get pushed out of their orbit. (Ionization) and create an ion pair, free e- and + charged atom

    Works by a sealed tube of gas absorbing the radiation. When radiation hits the particles, and they get ionized, the wire in the tube attracts and counts the electrons CPM (COUNTS PER MIN)

    Provides the exposure rate: (mR/h), the amount of radiation in the vicinity —> can be used to determine the dose rate, which is how much radiation is assumed to be absorbed by the person.

    Average background radiation is around 0.05 - 0.2 uSv and it comes from the earth’s atmosphere, cosmic rays, and nearby radioactive materials. Considered safe for both patients and healthcare providers. Annual occupational dose limit 50 uSv for whole-body exposure. The conversion from uSv to CPM is uSv 0.0082 = 1 CPM.

    X-Ray and Gamma: Highest Energy EM radiation, penetrates human tissue, is called penetrating radiation. Both are hazardous to humans, a dense material is needed to shield. Gamma radiation is easily detected.

    Beta: light short-range particles that can eject an electron. Can travel in the air, can penetrate the germinal layer of the skin, and cause skin injury. Some beta emitters are hard to detect due to their low energy. What GM can’t do: tell you the OG source of radiation, the amount of E it has or what type of radiation. Can be used for diagnostic and therapeutic purposes; most commonly used in iagnostic imaging and radiation therapy (gamma), computed tomography, radiography (X-rays), brachytherapy (beta)

    What does it mean to have high radiation? Increased use of imaging procedures(cumulative radiation), technical errors or malfunctions, inadequate maintenance of equipment, or nonadherence to radiation safety guidelines. Medical providers who work with radiation are allowed to experience twice as much radiation as the regular public.

    High radiation in the medical field can cause: Acute radiation syndrome (nausea, vomiting, hair loss, bone marrow damage, skin burns, etc), increased risk of cancer (uncontrolled growth of cancer cells due to mutations in the cells), genetic and hereditary effects and disorders, radiation-induced tissue damage, increased risk of radiation-induced illness (radiation fibrosis, cataracts, thyroid disorders)

    DHT22 Sensor: Tempature and Humdity

    Temperature: Heating, Ventilation, AC (HVAC), equipment malfunctions, other factors that can affect patient safety

    Optimal: 68-75 F; 20-24 C; Comfortable for both surgical team and patient

    Temperature too High: Patient Discomfort; Infection Risk: High temperatures can promote bacterial growth and increase the risk of infection in the surgical site; Surgical Team Discomfort

    Temperature too Low: Hypothermia Risk: slowed wound healing, cardiac problems, increased surgical complications

    Humidity:

    Optimal: 30-60%

    High Humidity: Infection Risk - can promote growth of microorganisms (bacteria and fungi), which can increase the risk of surgical site infections; Equipment Issues - can lead to equipment malfunctions, especially sensitive electronic and optical equipment

    Low Humidity: Patient Discomfort - can lead to dry, irritated mucous membranes and discomfort for both patient and surgical team; Wound Desiccation - can cause excessive drying of the surgical site, potentially hindering wound healing and increasing the risk of complications; Static Electricity - lead to an increase in static electricity, which can sensitive electronic equipment and create sparks that might be hazardous in an environment with oxygen-rich gasses

    LTR390 Sensor: UV Light

    UV (peak spectral response): 300 and 350 nm

    Monitoring UV exposure in Phototherapy: Phototherapy is sued for several medical conditions (neonatal jaundice, psoriasis, vitiligo, etc). This sensor can be used to monitor and control the dose of UV-A radiation that patients receive during treatment

    Sterilization: ~254 nm UV light is needed from (100-280 nm) to kill microorganisms. Many labs utilize UV light to sterilize their medical equipment in labs.

    UV Exposure in Labs: Labs that need UV light sources, that are found in equipment, can risk overexposure. UV sensor will measure the amount of UVA and UVB and the resulting data will alert if there is too much of either one

    UV Index Readings:

  • UVA (320 - 400 nm): Must be 1 milliwatt for cm^2 for over 8 hours
  • UVB (280 - 320 nm): 0.1 milliwatt/cm^2

    • The importance for lab personnel is that being exposed to UV sources intermittently is harmful, especially with UVB. Sometimes protective clothing is not enough. Its harmful to skin and can cause long-term skin damage and some skin cancers
